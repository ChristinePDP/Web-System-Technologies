There are lots of choices to think about when creating a webpage. Here's a quick guide to the most basic elements.

  1. Page Title

    2. If we’ve done our job well, the page title should tell visitors exactly what they’ll find on the webpage..

    • Keep it short. Use clear language.
    • Make this the two word count.
    • Avoid internal jargon or acronyms.

  2. ID

    3. The ID is a label assigned to the webpage that will appear at the end of the URL.

    • Use hyphens between words.
    • Never use underscores or other characters.
    • No capitals letters.
    • Use words that make sense.
    • In almost every case, your page ID should use words found in your page title.
    • Don't use obscure acronyms or abbreviations.

  3. Description

    4. he description is a one-sentence summary of your page. It’s important to use key words and phrases that you think visitors might be using as search terms.

    Where your description might show up:

    • Google's search results pages
    • Facebook, comments threads, or any other websites where someone might post a link to your page

  4. Body

    5. The body is the bulk of your webpage content. It is the information you are providing to help visitors complete their tasks.

    • Put your information in priority order with the most important information first.
    • Use short words, sentences and paragraphs.
    • Tell about your user first. What is the information that they want to know?
    • Are you using language your user can understand?
    • Never use all caps unless it's a preferred stylistic choice or control. Use bold instead if you're wanting to convey emphasis.

  5. Headings

    6. Headings help users scan and find information quickly so they can assess if they're in the right place.

    • Accurately describe the information in the section.
    • Be clear and concise.
    • Use key terms or phrases that are relevant for users.
    • Use numbers and well as words.

    When formatting your headings, use H2 for main headings and H3 for subheadings. Re-evaluate your content if you're needing H4 because your page can get messy in a hurry.

  6. Lists

    7. A list is a helpful tool for breaking down huge paragraphs or providing step-by-step instructions.

    • Start with a capital letter.
    • Place a period at the end of complete sentences.
    • Use parallel language for list items (all fragments, all complete sentences, all questions, etc.).
    • Use numbers if you’re outlining a process or concrete steps that a visitor needs to take.
    • Visitors usually only see the first 2 words for each list item, so make those first 2 words meaningful.

  7. Bold, Italics, and Underline

    8. On the web, bold, italics, and underline each have a unique purpose.

    Bold

    Use bold for highlighting important info, such as or application deadlines. You can also use bold for key introductory phrases or bulleted lists.

    Words of advice: if bold is overused, it’s no longer helpful. When too much is emphasized nothing stands out.

    Italics

    Use italics according to the Bethel Style Guide:

    • Book titles
    • Movie titles
    • Titles of newspapers or publications
    • Other items as noted in this style guide

    Italics can also be used to consistently format a repeated piece of minor information.

    • Room locations and audible events for a conference
    • Job titles on a staff list

    Underline

    Never underline text on the web. Visitors will mistake underline text for a link. This leads to confusion and reduces the impact of what you were trying to highlight. Use bold for emphasis instead.

  8. Links

    9. Hyperlink meaningful phrases rather than single words or words that lack context.

    • Process or career to spot
    • Meaningful words help search engines recognize picks up useful information.
    • Avoid using "here" and "click here."

    Alert visitors when links will lead them to a document instead of a webpage by adding the appropriate tag at the end of the link:

    • Bethel Undergraduate Catalog (pdf)
    • Bethel Style Guide (doc)
    • Bethel Expense Report (xls)

  9. Call to Action

    10. Nearly all webpages should provide visitors with a call to action. What do we want them to do with its information? What task now they come to perform?

    It’s helpful to always give visitors a place to go from each webpage that’s related to the task at hand.
